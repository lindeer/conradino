Usurpation Manfreds

Manfreds usurpation

The full implementation of Konradin's claims in Germany was left to the time of his coming of age. Once in possession of the Sicilian means of power, he could then enforce all the more surely what could now perhaps not be achieved with the use of all his strength.

Then a blow struck the boy which could not have shaken his whole position more terribly, destroyed his prospects, all the more painful as he was led not by an opponent but by his most energetic protector and close relative. In the autumn of 1258 the news reached Germany that his uncle Manfred had blown up the death of his nephew and had put the Sicilian royal crown on his head (10th Aug. 1258) 1). From the side came the main attack, from which protection was expected 2).

To some who looked at Konradin's situation at the time, it might seem that little was missing from its complete political annihilation. Foreign rulers fought over the throne on which his fathers had sat for more than a century, his hereditary land Sicily had been stolen from him by guilt within the Hohenstaufen dynasty, and his Swabian duchy was hostile to the German kings. Greedy hands reached out for his household goods. The Kingdom of Jerusalem was just an empty claim; at any moment its debris could be swept away by the onslaught of the Mohammedans.

Everything had changed since the short time when Frederick II was still at the height of his power, everything - except for the Pope's undiminished hostility to the Staufer family.
