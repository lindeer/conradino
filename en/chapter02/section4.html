Taking possession of the Swabian duchy, Swabian counts, lords, prelates, ministerials

Besitznahme des schwäbischen Herzogtums, schwäbische Grafen, Herren, Prälaten, Ministerialen

For the time being, the opposition between his two patrons did not come to the fore. Richard, who returned to his homeland after a short stay on the Rhine, certainly did not want to leave any open enemies behind in Germany, in fact he tried to captivate Ludwig through his own interest during his absence by giving him the imperial fiefs of Count von Dillingen until his return temporarily transferred to Germany 3). But a conflict was prepared which, with the increasing activity of the Staufer party, would sooner or later lead to an open outbreak.

Before that happened, Ludwig tried to give his nephew a more stable position by securing him the Duchy of Swabia. The ecclesiastical and secular lords there had grown tired of waiting for their King Alfons, but - in contrast to some of his other German supporters4) they had not converted to Richard, but had retained their neutrality. With them, especially with Bishop Eberhard von Konstanz, Ludwig entered into negotiations that led to a satisfactory conclusion. "Konradin, then ten years old, was supposed to formally take over power in Swabia, but for the time being stand under the tutelage of the Bishop of Constance for affairs there. 1) Ludwig himself would have aroused the mistrust and resistance of the nobility against himself if he ran the business himself.

In the spring of 1262 Konradin actually went to his duchy 2) and held a court day for the first time in Ulm from 3) on Whitsun and a second in Rotweil 4) at the beginning of August. After confirming the rights granted by his ancestors to the nearby Rotenmünster monastery, he moved on and met his uncle in Constance. Here, first of all, we get to know the names of the personalities who surrounded the young duke in these new and unusual circumstances and can look around them 5).

Never again has such an impressive number of Swabian counts gathered around Konradin as they did when they showed him their appreciation by their appearance. In addition to the Count Palatinate of Tubingen, Count Friedrich von Zollern and others, we also find Ulrich von Würtemberg among them, whose assistance with advice and deed was soon sought to secure a new concession, this time in money). But precisely the example of this Würtemberger suggests that Konradin could not find any lasting support for his ducal power in people of his own kind. These counts, who in the face of general confusion and legal uncertainty were well on their way to becoming their own masters , could have no immediate interest in establishing princely power over themselves. That the new duke was a boy might make it easier for them to agree, and also that he had prospects of the German royal crown; for an imperial power with whom they were friends could in turn protect them against the encroachments of the princes. To provide real support, but for the time being one was not inclined to do so without corresponding perks. As far as we know, Konradin only had a closer relationship with a few of them later on 1).

The small aristocratic families, who mostly lost out in competition, had to be more concerned with consolidating ducal power from which they could gain support. Hermann von Hürnheim, whose castle in the Ries was not far from Nördlingen, was now with him, now with that of his sons, an almost constant companion and trusted advisor to the young Staufer 2).

But he found his main support in the clerical dignitaries of Swabia on the one hand, in the ministeriality on the other: a political connection, as it was once brought into being on a larger scale by his ancestor Friedrich I, but shaken in later times in many ways was. Both estates were captivated by their own interest in the Duke. By granting the most powerful Swabian bishop and his followers decisive influence over the management of government affairs, one won his ambition for the cause of Konradin. On the other hand, the ducal court and possible political entanglements could only offer the ministerials, who were more concerned with court life and war travel than with economic gain, a desirable opportunity for their own activity 3). From both circles we come across some personalities, of whom we do not know much more than the mere name, some more clearly defined figures.

Bishop Eberhard II of Konstanz, Konradin's new guardian, was a warlike gentleman. His ancestors, the Truchsessen von Waldburg, had rendered important services to the Hohenstaufen dynasty; but he himself had taken the side of the opposing kings and the Pope 1). In these confused times - at first in bitter struggle - then in close alliance with Abbot Berthold of St. Gallen he had expanded his sphere of power ever further. Together they now ruled the land around Lake Constance and gave safe conduct to the Iller, where the Constance family touched the Augsburg diocese 2). - In these two men one does not yet feel anything of the new religious spirit which at that time filled the minds of the people and which already appeared powerfully in other places. While all around in the southern German lands Berthold von Regensburg awakened the conscience of sinners in the open air with his shocking penance sermons, and for the first time the Geissler gangs crossed southern Germany with torture and prayers 3), while the Dominican monk Albertus Magnus dem The Regensburg episcopate renounced in order to quietly process all medieval knowledge 4), as the Marner complained, “the stole was exchanged for the sword, the crook became a spear” 5). Her striving only went to “knighthood and worldly honor” 6). Of course, they understood each other all the better.

The picture of Berthold von Falkenstein, the abbot of St. Gallen, was drawn to us by a later chronicler of the monastery - perhaps with individual anecdotal embellishments and exaggerations, but essentially true to the truth and with gripping vividness 1) . One cannot deny admiration to this man of rare political talent, his brilliant knightly demeanor and his extraordinary generosity2), however much one feels rejected by his belligerence and ruthless hardness. - If he sets up lavish festivities on church holidays, gathers multitudes of knights around him and lets the fiddlers play, if he then improvises a feud immediately from the festival and leads the invited knights to battle, then he is in his element ! But he also knows how to secure the favor of the Pope in a difficult situation, to expand the territory of his abbey by fighting and buying, to collect rich taxes from the people of God and to hold down their indignation until he is finally dead ill himself. Only now does his system collapse. Lonely, hated by everyone, he dies, and when mass is sung for him, the people of God dance for joy in the streets.

Konradin stayed with these two men forever. in close contact. Very soon he and his episcopal guardian visited Abbot Berthold in the monastery of St. Gallen, was received with honor and stayed there for three days 3). Perhaps it was at this time that Mr. von Wengen, who lived in that area, praised the new moon in a song, which had risen so gloriously and would bring praise and honor to the country 4). The importance of Berthold von Falkenstein increased for the young duke because he was related to important dynasty families of Swabia and probably won some of them for him 1). From the privileges of the monasteries in the Konstanzer Sprengel 2), from the express emphasis that the bishop and abbot had advised to take a significant step3), the frequently recurring co-sealing of Konradin's documents 4) and the spiritual version of some of them one can see the decisive influence recognize these two men now and later during his entire stay in the Duchy of Swabia 5).

In addition to them, Abbot Rupert von Kempten takes a back seat). During that well-attended court day in Constance, to which this discussion is linked, Konradin renounced the bailiwick of the Kempten monastery for an annual fee of fifty marks 7). Whether the need for money, the attempt to win the abbot for himself, or finally the intercession of the Bishop of Constance led to this task of a not unimportant sovereign right, cannot be decided with certainty. - The influence of Bishop Hartmann von Augsburg on Konradin was initially still small. Only later would he have to deal with him, partly in a hostile, partly in a friendly manner.

The Reichsministerialiale Swabia, first and foremost several holders of imperial court offices: the Reichsmarschall von Pappenheim and the Reichsschenken von Limburg, next to them Swabian court officials: the Truchsessen from Waldburg and Warthusen, the taverns from Schmalnecke-Winterstetten, the treasurers from Ravensburg and von Bienburg, sooner or later we will find them all in the vicinity of Konradin 1), and their cosmopolitan, enterprising spirit, directed towards knighthood, women's service and poetry, seems to have determined the inclinations of the growing boy to an outstanding degree.

Naturally, he entered into closer personal relationships with his own ministerials, who were his loyal advisers and in some cases also the executive bodies of ducal policy. - Unfortunately, we are only very poorly informed about the extent and administration of the remaining Hohenstaufen goods. The old Staufer possessions in northern Swabia, Baden and Alsace, the Salic heritage in Franconia had been lost, with very few exceptions. What was left was, apart from the claims to some imperial estates and the Bamberg fiefs, basically the Old Welfish house ownership, increased by a few landscapes, on both banks of the Lech, in the Allgäu and on Lake Constance. Just as the above-mentioned Reichsministeriales almost all came from these areas 2), so do those ministerials and feudal men whom we get to know as the constant companions of Konradin: Volkmar von Kemnaten, Swiker von Mindelberg and Hermann von Hägenberg, - in addition to the Wittelsbach ministerial Heinrich von Eisolzried 3). The young duke later attached great importance to their advice, involved them in important matters 4) and even gave them major administrative tasks. In the beginning, of course, the Bavarian noble Albero von Bruckberg, who was probably already appointed by Duke Ludwig, was Konradins am Lech's procurator in Peiting Castle 1) and thus a senior civil servant for the provosts distributed throughout the country for the administration of justice and administration 2). Later the most important places in the Lech area Volkmar von Kemnaten were entrusted for temporary preservation 3).

In this man we may see one of the chief tutors of Conradin. He could hardly have found a more suitable one 4). His whole life compels us and already compelled his contemporaries to be recognized and respected 5). He knew how to harmoniously unite the chivalrous, art-loving spirit that characterized the first half of the century and the sober, practical sense of the later period. Equally valued by the feisty as a capable warrior, by the peace-loving as a legal referee, by the travelers as a hospitable patron 6), he had established an abundance of relationships in a life rich in experience that now seem to have created the mature man to the adviser of his master appear. As a servant of Herr von Ulten, who had acquired the allodes of the Margraves of Ronsberg (in the Lech region) by inheritance in addition to his Tyrolean possessions, he knew the conditions in Tyrol from his own experience and was recognized by the Counts of Gorizia and Tyrol as Shop steward consulted for important tasks 7). When Friedrich II then acquired these properties for his house, Volkmar entered into an employment relationship with the Staufers and was often in the vicinity of Konrad IV Umbrella Vogtei over the women's monastery Oberschönefeld transferred. His father-in-law had been a servant to the Bishop of Constance, and the town of Arbon on Lake Constance, which he inherited from the latter, was hard on the territory of the abbot of St. Gallen: a variety of relationships that could hardly be imagined more favorable for Konradin . The fact that we occasionally hear about damage that Volkmar inflicted on the Füssen Monastery is not a surprise to us at this time, when there was nothing without violence, even for the best. The fact that Konradin later appreciated the loyal service of this man, who was constantly in his environment with his son Marquard, is shown by a donation to the Oberschoenefeld monastery, where Volkmar's daughter Adelheid was abbess, in addition to many a fervent commission ); and a long stay of the young duke in the city of Arbon, where he and his entourage were probably entertained by Volkmar, tells us about the intimate personal relationship. Konradin later expressed the friendly memory of that time in a document in which he gave the Arbon community its own jurisdiction 2).
